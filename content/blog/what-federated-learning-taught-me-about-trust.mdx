I spent a few months building a privacy-preserving federated-learning pipeline for healthcare data. The headline result was a number — 91% accuracy without ever centralising the data. But the thing I actually took away wasn't the number.

## Learning without looking

Federated learning flips the usual deal. Instead of gathering everyone's data into one place, the model travels to the data, learns locally, and sends back only what it learned — never the records themselves.

> The most interesting constraint wasn't accuracy. It was: how little can the system know about a person and still be useful to them?

That constraint is really about **trust**. A hospital doesn't hand over its patients' records on a promise; it shouldn't have to. The architecture itself becomes the promise.

## The trade-off nobody escapes

The honest part is that there's always tension — between how much you communicate each round and how much you might leak through those updates. You don't solve it; you choose where to sit on the curve, on purpose, and you're clear about it.

I think that's the version of AI I want to keep working on: capable, but designed to know as little about people as it can get away with.

There's a question I keep returning to, no matter what I'm building: *will this make someone feel a little more seen than they did before?* It sounds soft for a computer-science student to admit, but it's the most useful design constraint I've found.

## The brief nobody writes down

Every project has the obvious requirements — it should work, it should be fast, it should ship. But underneath those there's a quieter brief that rarely gets written down: how should the person on the other side *feel* while using this?

> Technology is never neutral. It either makes someone feel capable, or it makes them feel small.

When I designed *On The Move*, the feature list said "ride matching." The real brief was: *a nervous first-time rider should feel safe getting into a stranger's car.* Once I held onto that, the interface decisions got easier — verification became reassurance, not friction.

## Small details, on purpose

Feeling seen lives in the small things — the empty state that doesn't blame you, the copy that sounds like a person, the loading moment that respects your time. None of it shows up in a spec. All of it shows up in how someone remembers the product.

That's the thread running through everything here: the research, the design, the NGO. I'm trying to build things that are quietly on your side.
